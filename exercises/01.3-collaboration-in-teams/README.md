# 01.3 Collaboration in Teams

Effective collaboration is the backbone of successful software development teams. In this lesson, you'll explore practical techniques to foster respect, share ideas clearly, and resolve conflicts constructively within your team.

## Establish a Shared Agenda

Starting meetings with a clear agenda helps keep discussions focused and inclusive. For example, a product team at Spotify might begin by having each member briefly state their main point. This ensures all voices are heard and the meeting stays on track.

> **Example:**
> "Let's each share our top priority for today's sprint before diving into the details."

## Use "I" Statements to Express Perspectives

When sharing your views, use **"I" statements** to communicate your feelings or concerns without assigning blame. This approach promotes openness and reduces defensiveness.

- Instead of saying: "You’re delaying the project," say:
  
  **"I feel concerned about the timeline if we add this feature."**

## Practice Active Listening

Active listening means fully concentrating on what others say and confirming your understanding by paraphrasing.

- **Paraphrase:** "So you’re suggesting we prioritize user feedback before launching?"
- This reduces misunderstandings and shows respect for your teammates' ideas.

## Resolve Conflicts with the Interest-Based Relational Approach

Focus on underlying interests rather than fixed positions to find common ground.

- If two developers disagree on implementation, ask each to explain their goals (e.g., performance vs. maintainability).
- Then, work together to find solutions that address both interests.

## Use Time-Boxed Speaking Rounds

To ensure everyone has a chance to contribute, use time-boxed rounds where each person speaks without interruption.

- This prevents louder voices from dominating and encourages balanced participation.
- Agile retrospectives at companies like Instagram often use this pattern.

## Encourage Solution-Oriented Discussions

When conflicts arise, focus on proposing solutions rather than just pointing out problems.

- For example, if there's disagreement over code style, suggest adopting a shared linter configuration to align the team.
